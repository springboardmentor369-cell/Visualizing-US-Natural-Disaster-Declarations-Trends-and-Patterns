EDA-Driven Answer

Based on exploratory data analysis of disaster declarations from 1953 to 2025, the following three insights emerge as most critical for FEMA-level decision-making:

1. Disaster risk shows a long-term upward trend with extreme peak years
Year-wise analysis indicates that disaster declarations have increased over time, with significant spikes during certain historical periods. These peaks highlight the need for planning based on worst-case scenarios, not just average annual activity.

2. Disaster impact is geographically concentrated, not uniform
A limited number of states account for a disproportionate share of disaster declarations. This suggests that targeted regional preparedness is more effective than uniform national strategies.

3. Floods and severe storms dominate disaster declarations
EDA shows that floods and storms are the most frequently declared disaster types across decades. This indicates that preventive infrastructure investments, such as flood control and early-warning systems, can significantly reduce future impact.

Implication
These insights support a shift toward data-driven preparedness planning, enabling FEMA to prioritize regions, allocate budgets efficiently, and move from reactive response to proactive risk mitigation.
